Dalio's View on Market Bubble, Credit Cycle Dynamics, and Global Power Shifts

1) Current Market Environment: Signs of a Bubble, but Not an Imminent Crash

Ray Dalio indicates that todayâ€™s financial markets are definitely in a bubble. However, he emphasizes that this does not necessarily mean a near-term market crash. The reason is that liquidity remains abundant, and credit markets are still functioning normally. Historically, bubbles do not burst merely due to high valuations; they tend to collapse only when liquidity tightens and investors are forced to convert paper wealth into cash.

2) Equity Wealth-to-Money Ratio at Alarmingly High Levels

According to Dalio, the ratio of U.S. equity wealth to total money supply has reached extremely elevated levels. These levels closely resemble two major historical bubble episodes:

  • The late 1920s, just before the Great Depression, and
  • The late 1990s dot-com bubble.

Such elevated ratios suggest excessive financial wealth relative to actual money available to support valuations, which increases systemic vulnerability during liquidity contractions.

3) Long-Term Return Expectations

Dalio further suggests that due to these extreme conditions, real stock market returns over the next decade could be close to zero. This implies that traditional equity-heavy portfolios might experience low real returns, despite nominal gains, especially in an inflationary or tightening-liquidity environment.

4) Integrating Dalioâ€™s View with Credit Cycle Theory

Recently, I learned more about the credit cycle, which connects closely with Dalioâ€™s insights. During the late stages of a credit cycle, default rates begin to rise, credit becomes more expensive or scarce, and the value of corporate credit instruments declines. Historically, many major financial crises originate in the credit markets, not equity markets.

This underscores the importance of:

  • Maintaining cash reserves or liquidity,

So that when defaults peak and asset prices drop, we are positioned to buy undervalued assets during the recovery phase.

Therefore, understanding and timing the credit cycle is critical not only for risk management but also for capitalizing on distressed opportunities.

But since the market kept testing the ceiling and doubting Japanâ€™s credit, the BoJ kept raising the ceiling and eventually removed it, causing yields to surge.
