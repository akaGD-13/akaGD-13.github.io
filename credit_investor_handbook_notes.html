Part One: Building Blocks of the Leveraged Credit Markets

1. A Brief History of Leveraged Credit Markets

üîπ Debt Basics

  • Pros: Does not require giving up ownership; interest payments are tax-deductible, whereas dividends are not.
  • Cons: Requires timely payments of interest/principal; failure to do so can lead to bankruptcy.

üîπ What is the Leveraged Credit Market?

Refers to corporate bonds and loans issued by companies below investment grade (Baa3 / BBB-). Also known as:
High Yield (HY), Sub-investment grade, Speculative grade, Junk debt.

üîπ Three Major Segments of Non-Investment-Grade Debt

  1. High Yield (HY) Bonds
  2. Broadly Syndicated Loans (BSL) ‚Äî leveraged loans / bank loans / bank debt
  3. Private Credit / Direct Lending

üîπ 1 High Yield (HY) Bonds

Michael Milken contributed to the corporate bond field

  • Created a new source of capital for the huge universe of companies without investment-grade ratings.
  • Fueled economic growth and job creation by enabling companies to invest in growth initiatives.
  • Provided investors, including endowment and pension funds, with higher-yielding investment opportunities.
  • Provided a catalyst for corporate governance improvements by facilitating takeovers of under-managed companies.

üîπ 2 Broadly Syndicated Loans (BSL) / Leveraged Loans

Key differences vs HY bonds:

  • Less liquid; generally only purchased by Qualified Institutional Buyers (QIBs)
  • Secured by collateral ‚Äî sits higher in the capital structure
  • Loans are not securities ‚Äî less subject to securities laws like insider trading

CLOs (Collateralized Loan Obligations)

A CLO manager bundles 100+ leveraged loans into a pooled vehicle and issues securities (tranches) to investors from AAA to equity.

Why they matter: The strength or weakness of the CLO market (the ‚ÄúCLO bid‚Äù) directly impacts financing costs for LBO transactions.

üîπ 3 Private Credit / Direct Lending

Direct lenders (Apollo, Ares, Blackstone, Golub, Owl Rock, etc.) increasingly compete with banks in middle-market lending.

Why it's growing:

  • Senior secured debt has provided excellent risk‚Äêadjusted returns through various market cycles.
  • The floating‚Äêrate structure of private credit insulates investors from increasing interest rates.
  • Well‚Äêstructured senior secured debt has historically fared better than most asset classes in a recession.
2. Patterns Across Credit Cycles

üîÑ Four Phases

  • Expansion: growth and easier credit; defaults remain low.
  • Downturn: defaults rise as growth slows and liquidity tightens.
  • Repair: defaults begin to decline as balance sheets are fixed.
  • Recovery: credit standards relax; risk appetite returns.

üìâ Notable Downturns

  • 1997‚Äì1998: Asian Financial Crisis and contagion across emerging markets.
  • 2000‚Äì2002: Dot-com/TMT bubble burst. Normalization late 2002 driven by:
    • Monetary policy: Fed cut the funds rate from 6% (Jan 2001) to 1.8% (Dec 2001).
    • Fiscal policy: 2001 Economic Growth and Tax Relief Reconciliation Act lowered rates and raised the child tax credit.
    • Diminishing fear of follow-on attacks: reduced risk aversion post-9/11.
    • Enhanced regulation: bipartisan reforms after accounting scandals (e.g., Enron) improved reporting accuracy.
  • 2008‚Äì2009: Global Financial Crisis/Great Recession; mortgage-backed securities at the core.
  • 2020: Global pandemic and sudden-stop recession.

4. Primer on Leveraged Buyouts (LBOs)

LBO: is the acquisition of a company by a private equity (PE) firm using a significant amount of debt relative to equity.

  • Financial Sponsor / Sponsor / Financial Buyer: the PE firm
  • The target: the company being acquired
  • Sponsor financing / Acquisition financing: the debt raised to fund the LBO
  • Portfolio company: a company acquired by a PE fund

60-75% normally

The credit markets have also evolved to include debt financing options with lower cash servicing requirements and fewer borrower restrictions, such as:

  • Paid‚Äêin‚Äêkind (PIK) coupons: allow the borrower to defer cash interest payments by paying with new debt rather than cash, thus preserving liquidity
  • Covenant‚Äêlight (‚Äúcov‚Äêlite‚Äù) loans: do not require maintenance of financial ratios such as leverage and coverage
  • Recurring revenue loans: lender focuses on revenue rather than cash flow

A PE firm raises multiple funds to make investments. Each fund typically has a 10‚Äêyear life. The PE firm acquires companies in the first four to six years (the investment period). During the last four to six years, it monetizes those investments (the harvest period).

In the investment period, the PE sponsor has the goals summarized in

  1. Identifying/sourcing investment opportunities
    • Auctions
    • Proprietary deals
  2. Negotiating the acquisition
  3. Financing the debt component and closing the deal

After making an acquisition, the sponsor focuses on harvesting in two ways, summarized in:

  1. Improving the company
  2. Returning capital to investors: dividend, sales of the business, IPO‚Ä¶

Sell-Side M&A process:

  1. The target selects a sell‚Äêside advisor.
  2. The investment bank performs due diligence, including a preliminary valuation of the business.
  3. The investment bank in consultation with the company identifies a buyer universe
  4. The bank prepares and distributes marketing materials to the buyer universe.
  5. The bank runs the auction.
  6. The target and its advisor pick a winner.
  7. The acquisition closes

Part Two: Seven-Step Process of Evaluating a Debt Investment

A structured framework for analyzing performing (non-distressed) debt instruments.

Traditional "5 Cs of Credit"

  1. Character: leadership quality, reputation, strategy.
  2. Capacity: ability to generate cash to service debt.
  3. Capital: equity cushion relative to debt.
  4. Conditions: competitive landscape and industry context.
  5. Collateral: quality of assets supporting the debt.

The Seven Steps

  1. Sources and Uses (originations): why financing is needed and how it is funded.
  2. Qualitative Analysis: industry outlook, business strategy, management, ESG.
  3. Financial Statement Analysis: profitability, cash flow and liquidity, capital structure.
  4. Forecasting: key drivers and assumptions; base, upside, and downside cases.
  5. Corporate Valuation: comps, precedent transactions, DCF, liquidation analysis.
  6. Structuring and Documentation: economics, structure/collateral, covenants.
  7. Investment Recommendation: thesis, risks/mitigants, relative value, credit memo.

  3. Senior unsecured high yield bonds
  4. Subordinated, or mezzanine debt

Chapter 7: Step 3(a) ‚Äî Financial Statement Analysis: Profitability

profitability analysis: 6 sub-steps

  1. Adjust the reported income statement to remove nonrecurring gains and losses using data from the income statement, footnotes, and management's commentary. Adjustments for nonrecurring gains and losses will be an exhibit to your investment memo.
  2. Create a summary adjusted income statement as shown in Figure 7.6. For a noncyclical business, three to five years of historical information is adequate. For a cyclical industry, you should include enough history to capture a complete economic cycle, including at least one recession.
    EBITDA stands for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization
  3. Analyze earnings quality. Are there any red flags that make you question the GAAP income statement? Is the reported income statement a good reflection of economic reality, or is it potentially misleading?
    • Potential dealer dumping
    • Sales growth versus order growth
    • Impact of foreign exchange
    • Impact of acquisition
  4. Create summary adjusted income statements for the company's most direct peer group / competitors. Draw conclusions
  5. Did the company make, beat, or miss expectations (yours and the analyst community's)?
    • How did the company perform relative to its peer group?
    • Are results good or bad in an absolute sense?
    • What are the critical drivers of performance?
    • What other conclusions can you draw that are potentially important to making an investment recommendation?
  6. Make a list of follow‚Äêup questions for management and other due diligence items.
Chapter 8: Step 3(b) ‚Äî Financial Statement Analysis: Cash Flow and Liquidity
  1. Perform a static analysis
  2. Input the debt maturity schedule for the next five years
  3. Calculate free cash flow (FCF)
  4. Analyze the cash cycle and liquidity implications of future changes in working capital
  5. Assess the company's ability to raise additional capital and refinance debt as it comes due
  6. Analyze trade creditor relationships
  7. Determine the company's ability to conserve cash by managing capex
  8. Note other levers to improve liquidity
  9. Draw conclusions from your work
  10. Make a list of questions for management and other due diligence follow‚Äêups

  2. Calculate credit ratios

Chapter 10: Step 4 ‚Äî Forecasting

Credit investment recommendations typically include three forecasts:

  • Management or consensus case
  • Base case
  • Downside cases

Preparing a forecast:

  1. Input historical financial statements (the process outlined in Chapter 7).
  2. Set up an assumption page detailing the drivers and the cells of the model that are sensitized to run cases.
  3. Forecast the income statement.
  4. Forecast the balance sheet excluding cash and debt, as both depend on cash flow.
  5. Forecast the cash flow statement and link it to the balance sheet to determine the amount of cash and debt iteratively.
  6. Perform sensitivity analysis by changing assumptions to run different cases.
  7. Analyze and summarize the outputs of the model on a summary page that includes the key financial ratios discussed in previous chapters.
  8. When appropriate, assign probabilities to all cases.

Chapter 12: Step 6 ‚Äî Structuring and Documentation
  1. Amount of bond or loan outstanding (face value or principal amount)
  2. Debt type
  3. Maturity date
  4. Required amortization and excess cash flow (ECF) sweep
    • Amortization refers to any required repayment prior to maturity
    • The ECF provision is more flexible than amortization. If the company produces positive cash flow as defined in the credit agreement for the period, it must use a portion of this cash flow, typically ranging from 25% to 75%, to sweep‚Äîthat is, pay down‚Äîthe loan. In comparison, amortization is a contractual obligation to pay down debt even if the company does not produce adequate cash flow to do so.
  5. Coupon
    • Most leverage loans have floating rates (SOFR + spread, common to have a SOFR floor)
    • Coupon pricing grids
    • Coupon step-up provision
    • Payment in kind interest (PIK)
  6. Up‚Äêfront fees or original issue discount (OID)
    • typically 2% up-front fee ($98) for new loan
    • New bond is OID
  7. Prepayment penalties/call protection:
    • ‚ÄúThe debt instrument can never trade above par, but it can trade below par. If the company's credit profile improves, its debt is less risky‚Äîbut without prepayment penalties, you won't get the benefit of that improvement because the borrower will refinance its debt at a lower coupon. The same holds true for the level of risk‚Äêfree rates; if Treasury rates go down, a borrower can refinance its fixed‚Äêrate debt with lower‚Äêcoupon debt if there is no penalty for doing so.
    • Non-call provision: expensive make-whole premium
    • Hard call protection
    • Soft call protection
    • No call protection: rare
  8. Change of control put
  9. Contractual and structural subordination (will be covered in Chapter 17)
  10. Collateral
  11. Covenants: contracts / Agreements to protect creditors
    • Maintenance / incurrence covenants
  12. Amendments
Chapter 13: Step 7 ‚Äî Investment Recommendation and Credit Committee Memo

A good memo includes:

  • A clear, concise, up‚Äêfront recommendation that incorporates relative value versus other investment opportunities;
  • A clearly articulated investment thesis;
  • The significant risks to the investment with potential mitigants;
  • Enough qualitative and quantitative analyses to support the investment thesis and allow for a meaningful debate of the merits and risks of the investment opportunity.
