Credit Modeling Overview

1. Credit Modeling Overview

The deck distinguishes three broad areas where credit models are used:

  • Loan credit analysis and management (banking book): assessing the creditworthiness of borrowers and managing loan portfolios.
  • Trading book credit risk: modeling defaults and credit migrations for traded instruments.
  • Fixed income investment: analyzing credit spreads and their drivers for bond and spread products.

Each area has a different objective and therefore uses different models and risk-management practices. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

2. Loan Credit Analysis & Risk Management

2.1 Loan Credit Analysis (Banking Book)

Loan credit analysis is primarily relevant for the banking book, where the goal is to evaluate a borrower before making a loan.

Key focus areas differ for commercial and retail borrowers:

  • Commercial loans: balance sheet strength, cash flow generation, market performance, and overall company performance.
  • Retail loans: credit scores (e.g., FICO), tax returns, existing indebtedness, savings, job security, and geographic or demographic factors.

2.2 Loan Credit Risk Management

Once loans are in the portfolio, risk is managed differently than in a trading book:

  • Analyze the borrower carefully before granting credit.
  • Adjust loan pricing (interest rate) to compensate for borrower risk.
  • Avoid excessive concentration in specific borrowers or sectors.
  • After origination, monitor collateral values, payment timeliness, and covenants; restructure loans if necessary, and seize collateral as a last resort.
3. Trading Book Credit Risk & Fixed Income Investment

3.1 Trading Book Credit Risk

Trading book credit risk models focus on default events and credit quality changes. They typically analyze:

  • Default probabilities.
  • Rating transitions and migration matrices.
  • Recovery rates given default.
  • Credit spread behavior (distinct from pure spread volatility, which overlaps with general market risk).

Two main classes of default models are referenced: reduced-form models and structural models.

3.2 Fixed Income Investment

For fixed income investors, the central variable is the credit spread. Typical analyses include:

  • Volatility and term structure of spreads.
  • Impact of spread movements on bond prices.
  • Links between credit spreads and macro or market factors (equity index levels, interest rates, volatility indices, etc.).
  • Statistical relationships used in relative-value and factor models.

Modeling approaches often use regressions or other statistical tools to link spreads with explanatory variables.

Default Modeling Overview

4. Default Modeling Frameworks

4.1 Traditional Taxonomy: Reduced-Form vs Structural Models

Historically, default models were divided into two categories:

  • Reduced-form models: model the timing of default directly via an exogenous process (often with an exponentially distributed default time), without modeling the firm’s asset value explicitly.
  • Structural models: model firm value as a continuous process and define default as the first time that assets fall below a default barrier (e.g., the level of liabilities).

4.2 Contemporary View

Modern classification focuses more on information content than on strict model form:

  • At one end, reduced-form models assume limited information about firm fundamentals; default time is unpredictable and described via a hazard-rate process.
  • At the other end, structural models assume rich information about the firm, so default can be anticipated when asset value approaches the barrier.

In practice, real-world models lie on a continuum between these extremes, adjusting assumptions about information and uncertainty.

5. Reduced-Form Models & Survival Analysis

5.1 Default Time & Survival Probability

Reduced-form models treat default time as a random stopping time. The key functions are:

  • τ: default time.
  • s(t): survival probability, i.e., probability of no default before time t.
  • p(t): default time probability density.
  • λ(t): hazard rate, the instantaneous default intensity at time t.

Relationships from survival analysis connect these quantities, for example (schematically):

  • s(t) = 1 − CDF(t)
  • p(t) = − d s(t) / dt
  • λ(t) = p(t) / s(t)

5.2 Constant Hazard Rate Example

With a constant hazard rate λ:

  • Survival: s(t) = e−λt.
  • Density: p(t) = λ e−λt.
  • Expected default time: E[τ] = 1/λ; variance: 1/λ².

This corresponds to an exponential distribution: default time is memoryless and unpredictable, a common assumption in basic intensity models.

5.3 Survival Curves (Graph Slide)

One slide shows several survival probability curves for different constant hazard rates (e.g., 50, 200, 500, 1000 bps). Higher hazard rates cause the survival curve to fall more quickly toward zero, illustrating how more credit risk compresses the expected lifetime of the obligor.

6. Pricing & Spread–Hazard Conversions

6.1 Valuing a Risky Cash Flow

Consider a risky payment of 1 dollar at time T, with recovery rate R if default occurs. Loss given default (LGD) is 1 − R.

In a reduced-form setting under the risk-neutral measure, the value decomposes into:

  • A survival leg (payment if no default before T).
  • A default leg (recovery payment if default occurs before T).

Using survival probability s(t) and hazard rate λ(t), the bond price can be expressed as discounted expectations of these legs.

7. Time-Dependent Hazard: Properties & Uses

7.1 General Properties

When hazard rates vary over time, default times are modeled using a non-homogeneous Poisson process. Important points:

  • Waiting times between potential default events are independent but not identically distributed when the intensity changes over time.
  • The process is no longer memoryless; knowing that an obligor has survived to a given time can change the conditional probability of default.
  • Piecewise-constant hazard rates are commonly used to calibrate to CDS spreads at specific maturities.
  • The survival curve s(t) is monotone decreasing with non-negative intensity λ(t).

7.2 Utility of Default Time Distributions

Time-dependent intensity models are applied to:

  • CDS pricing under independence assumptions.
  • Extension to risky bond pricing.
  • CVA calculations when portfolio exposure is assumed independent of default time (no wrong-way risk).
  • Market risk analysis by shocking hazard rates or spreads to measure P&L sensitivity.

7.3 Extension: Stochastic Intensities

The deck briefly notes that reduced-form models can be extended by making the hazard rate itself stochastic. Model behavior then depends on the dynamics chosen for the intensity process (e.g., mean-reversion, volatility).

8. Spread Modeling

8.1 Decomposing the Credit Spread

The spread on a risky bond can be decomposed into:

  • Real default component derived from hazard rates and LGD (sometimes called the “default spread”).
  • Market price of risk component which compensates investors for bearing systematic or model risk, beyond expected losses.

8.2 Mapping Default Probabilities to Spreads

Given risk-neutral default probabilities (via a survival curve), we can derive the credit spread consistent with observed prices:

  • Start from the risky cash-flow valuation (survival leg + default leg).
  • Express the result as a discount factor with an effective risky rate r + S, where S is the credit spread.
  • Rearrange to obtain S as a function of LGD and survival probability.

8.3 Real-World vs Risk-Neutral Spreads

A final slide presents the idea that the observed spread consists of:

  • S = S_d + S_m, where S_d is the spread implied by real-world default risk and S_m captures the “market price of risk”.
  • Empirical work suggests that S_m is often proportional to spread volatility, so that S_m = m σ, where m is the price of spread risk per unit of volatility.
