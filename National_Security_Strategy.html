National Security Strategy (2025) – Notes

I. Introduction – What Is American Strategy?

1. How American “Strategy” Went Astray

  • Strategy should connect ends and means with clear priorities, not be a wish list.
  • Post–Cold War elites assumed permanent U.S. global domination was desirable and feasible.
  • U.S. foreign policy overextended:
    • Took on global burdens with little connection to core U.S. interests.
    • Tried to fund a large welfare–administrative state and a huge global security role at the same time.
    • Backed globalism and “free trade” that hollowed out U.S. industry and the middle class.
    • Allowed allies to offload defense costs and drag the U.S. into peripheral conflicts.
    • Gave excessive power to international institutions that undermined national sovereignty.
  • Result: pursuit of an impossible goal while weakening the very foundations of U.S. power.

2. President Trump’s Necessary, Welcome Correction

  • Trump’s first term is presented as a course correction:
    • Restored border control and redirected the military to defend U.S. sovereignty.
    • Invested heavily in the armed forces and rebuilt alliances with higher allied defense spending.
    • Used tariffs and energy policy to revive American industry and independence.
  • Diplomatic achievements: negotiated peace or de-escalation in eight conflicts across multiple regions.
  • Second term aims to:
    • Define what the U.S. should want.
    • Identify means available.
    • Unify ends and means into a coherent National Security Strategy.
II. What Should the United States Want?

1. What Do We Want Overall?

  • Ensure survival and safety of the U.S. as a sovereign republic that protects citizens’ natural rights.
  • Protect territory, people, economy, and way of life from:
    • Military attack and espionage.
    • Predatory trade and economic coercion.
    • Drug and human trafficking.
    • Destructive propaganda and cultural subversion.
  • Achieve full control of borders, immigration system, and transportation networks.
  • Build resilient infrastructure able to withstand disasters and foreign threats.
  • Maintain the world’s strongest military:
    • Lethal, technologically advanced, able to win quickly and decisively.
    • Culture of pride and confidence in mission.
  • Maintain a robust nuclear deterrent and next-generation missile defenses (“Golden Dome”).
  • Preserve and strengthen:
    • World’s most dynamic economy and industrial base.
    • World-leading energy sector.
    • Scientific and technological leadership, with IP protected from theft.
    • Soft power based on confidence in America’s greatness and decency.
  • Restore cultural and spiritual health:
    • Pride in national history and heroes.
    • Optimism about a “new golden age.”
    • Strong traditional families and high labor-force participation.

2. What Do We Want In and From the World?

  • Western Hemisphere
    • Stability and governance sufficient to limit mass migration.
    • Cooperation against cartels and transnational crime.
    • Prevent hostile powers from controlling strategic assets or locations.
    • Assert a “Trump Corollary” to the Monroe Doctrine.
  • Indo-Pacific / Global Economy
    • Stop foreign damage to the U.S. economy while keeping the region free and open.
    • Preserve freedom of navigation and secure supply chains and critical materials.
  • Europe
    • Support allies in defending freedom and security.
    • Encourage revival of Western civilizational identity and self-confidence.
  • Middle East
    • Prevent hostile powers from dominating the region and energy chokepoints.
    • Avoid open-ended “forever wars.”
  • Technology & Standards
    • Ensure U.S. leadership in AI, biotech, quantum computing and associated global standards.
  • These are defined as core, vital national interests that must be prioritized above others.
III. Means and Strategy (Tools, Principles, Regions)

A. America’s Available Means

  • Political system that can still correct course.
  • Largest and most innovative economy; leverage via access to U.S. markets.
  • Dominant financial system and reserve-currency dollar.
  • World-leading technology sector supporting both economy and military.
  • Most capable military and extensive alliance network.
  • Favorable geography and natural resources; secure from land invasion.
  • Soft power and global cultural influence.
  • Patriotism and will of the American people.
  • Domestic agenda further strengthens means:
    • Restores competence and merit; removes DEI-type practices.
    • Unleashes energy production.
    • Reindustrializes economy and shortens supply chains.
    • Implements tax cuts and deregulation.
    • Invests in emerging tech and basic science.

B. Strategic Principles (“America First”)

1. Core Principles

  • Focused national interest: not everything is a vital interest.
  • Peace through strength: military, economic, technological, and cultural power deter conflict.
  • Predisposition to non-intervention: high bar for intervening in other nations’ affairs.
  • Flexible realism: work pragmatically with different regimes without trying to remake them.
  • Primacy of nations: nation-state is core political unit; sovereignty should be protected.
  • Sovereignty & respect: resist foreign censorship, influence operations, and immigration manipulation.
  • Balance of power: prevent any adversary from achieving regional or global dominance.
  • Pro-American worker: policy favors broad-based prosperity, not just aggregate GDP growth.
  • Fairness in alliances and trade: end free-riding and persistent imbalances.
  • Competence and merit: preserve them as core civilizational advantages; resist ideologies that undermine them.

2. Priority Themes

  • End of mass migration: border security as primary national security task.
  • Protection of rights and liberties: no abuse of state power under pretexts like “protecting democracy.”
  • Burden-sharing and burden-shifting: allies must take primary responsibility for their regions and raise defense spending (e.g., NATO 5% pledge).
  • Realignment through peace: presidential diplomacy to resolve conflicts and strengthen U.S. influence.
  • Economic security:
    • Balanced, reciprocal trade; oppose dumping and unfair barriers.
    • Secure access to critical supply chains and materials.
    • Reindustrialization and reshoring of strategic production.
    • Revitalized defense industrial base, including low-cost and high-end capabilities.
    • Energy dominance in oil, gas, coal, nuclear; rejection of “Net Zero” ideology.
    • Preserve and grow U.S. dominance in global finance and capital markets.

C. Regional Strategies

1. Western Hemisphere – “Trump Corollary”

  • Reassert Monroe Doctrine; deny adversaries strategic footholds.
  • “Enlist and Expand”:
    • Enlist partners to control migration, fight cartels, and build stability.
    • Expand U.S. partnerships, trade, and investment; encourage near-shoring.
  • Adjust U.S. military posture; strengthen naval and Coast Guard presence; offensive strategy vs. major cartels.
  • Use commercial diplomacy, tariffs, and finance tools to secure supply chains and counter Chinese influence.

2. Asia – Economic Competition & Deterrence

  • Recognizes misjudgments about China’s integration into a “rules-based order.”
  • Goal: rebalance economic relationship and preserve U.S. preeminence in Indo-Pacific.
  • End predatory practices, IP theft, supply-chain coercion, fentanyl precursor exports, and propaganda.
  • Coordinate with allies whose combined GDP rivals global majority; deepen ties with India and the Quad.
  • Invest in key military/dual-use technologies and deregulate to keep U.S. innovation edge.
  • Deterrence:
    • Prevent conflict over Taiwan; maintain ability to deny aggression in the First Island Chain.
    • Keep South China Sea open and toll-free.
    • Push allies (Japan, Korea, Australia, Taiwan) to spend more and develop relevant capabilities.

3. Europe – Promoting European Greatness

  • Identifies deeper European issues:
    • Economic decline and over-regulation.
    • Civilizational crisis due to migration, low birthrates, censorship, and loss of national identity.
  • U.S. aims:
    • Negotiate end to Ukraine war and reestablish stability with Russia.
    • Help Europe stand as aligned sovereign nations capable of self-defense.
    • Encourage democratic revival and patriotic parties.
    • Expand fair transatlantic trade and cooperation.
    • Discourage endless NATO expansion; push Europe to counter hostile economic practices.

4. Middle East – Shift Burden, Build Peace

  • Region less central as U.S. becomes energy independent, but still strategically important.
  • Iran weakened; Gaza ceasefire and hostage release credited to Trump diplomacy.
  • Future vision:
    • Middle East as partner in investment, energy, AI, and defense tech.
    • No more large-scale nation-building wars; focus on counter-terrorism and deterrence.
    • Expand Abraham Accords and maintain security of energy routes and Israel.

5. Africa – From Aid to Investment

  • Shift from ideology-driven, aid-heavy approach to trade and investment.
  • Prioritize partnerships with capable, reliable states.
  • Focus areas:
    • Conflict resolution and prevention in key hotspots.
    • Energy projects and critical minerals development.
    • Use U.S. nuclear, LPG, and LNG tech to create profitable ventures and strategic leverage.
  • Avoid long-term U.S. military entanglements while watching Islamist terrorism risks.
